# The Problem I'm Ready For

## 1. Who am I

**Through-line:** I'm an insight-finder and director-of-others, not a solo executor. My failure mechanism is one thing wearing three names — solution-first, partial listening, business-math over user-truth — all of them are acting on incomplete information because patience runs out before proof arrives. At my best: a trusted team, autonomy to choose my approach, room to go deep instead of fast or cheap. The fix I need from my environment is the mirror of the failure: outside anchors, short distance to source of truth, and a business win structurally baked into the user's win rather than fought for against it — no fight-or-flight forcing the shortcut. Resolved: the anchor isn't a superior, it's juniors — people deeply vested in the problem, deeper than me, who question me on goal/audience/KPI. In every best-case, I brute-force first, but respect for these people lets me actually take their feedback and refine. Involving more people and taking feedback from juniors, specifically, is the recipe — not deference to authority, deference to whoever is closer to the problem than I am.

- What do I actually know how to do, today, without hand-waving — not what I aspire to be good at?
  - I can nurture people and direct talented people to follow a solid process to a conclusion — that's a real, practiced skill, and one I can still get better at. Doing it alone, solo, I struggle. So the model that fits me is co-working — direction + others' hands — not solo execution.
- Where does my instinct to solve problems come from, and where does it show up outside of work?
  - I'm a problem solver — someone hands me food for thought and I'll turn it over day and night; when I don't have one, I go seek one. My default move is "what's wrong with the world" → problem → solve. But choosing the right problem is harder than solving one well, and sometimes what I solve is self-serving — something I cooked up, or something someone else's goal disguised as mine.
  - Since coming across Jürgen Klopp it's felt like a calling: I'll always be better mentoring people than doing it myself. I've been nurturing people around me since 3rd grade — and I was also always the smartest kid in the class, still am the smartest in the room. I see things nobody else does; I find insights and signals. I don't think I pick the wrong insights or signals. What I do with them — because of my own biases and constraints — is where it goes wrong. And sometimes I throw away insights staring me right in the face.
- What's the honest pattern in my failures — not the story I tell about them, but what repeats?
  - **Solution-first, then reverse-engineered problem.** I form a candidate answer early (e.g. dynamic correlations as the goal, or whatever a stakeholder hands me), then bend the problem definition to fit it — instead of the reverse. Conviction arrives before proof.
  - **Partial listening.** I hear enough of what someone says to pattern-match to an idea I already have, then push that idea back at them instead of continuing to extract their actual problem. Small vantage point + reaching for answers early = the failure.
  - **Unresolved tensions I oscillate on instead of balancing:** artist vs scientist, data-driven vs user-driven, ease of building vs solving the real problem, speed vs depth, thinking alone in a room vs going out and talking to people. In some journeys I've genuinely improved; in others I cycle between following a real process and just randomly doing things — following one part of the process, dropping another. Over time I reach some depth, some directional wins — but it still feels incomplete, imperfect. I'm smarter than most people around me and can bring real focus to what matters — but I can also get lost in my own bullshit.
- When I've been at my best, what was true about the problem I was working on?
  - So the strength to keep honing is the hunt for the right insights and signals — trust my foresight there. What I have to build is patience: not giving in to my own constraints and biases, cooking things well, listening before speaking, absorbing before acting, and not letting fear hide risks from me.
  - Concrete moments to mine for the pattern: rebuilding social discovery / redesigning the reccos game with Avantika & Diksha; community board with Prateek; the delivery engine at Thrive; the ezee webhook and its downstream issues at goSTOPS; the Dec-Mar strategic roadmap at goSTOPS.
  - What's consistent across all of them: a team I could trust, space to choose my own approach, feeling trusted — and I took control to solve it deeply, not fast or cheaply. Depth over speed, trust over oversight, autonomy over instruction.
- What do I need to be true about a problem for me to stay engaged with it past the first excitement?
  - It has to give me a vantage point where I imagine less and get closer to the source of truth. It should excite me to go talk to the people involved. I face it myself, or people around me — wherever I end up being — face it. Truth-seeking has to be easy and primary, not a chore. The need to get to an answer fast can't force me to fill in gaps with assumption.
  - Correction: this isn't only a property of the problem — it's a property of the environment I'm in. Distance from the source of the problem, and speed-to-answer vs. truth-seeking, are set by the environment (team, org, context) more than by the problem itself. So the filter is really: pick environments with short distance to source and no forced trade of truth for speed — the problem then inherits that.
  - Given I can get lost in my own bullshit, what I need from the problem and the environment is: **outside anchors** — people who hold me accountable; problems that can have a solid, solved definition (not endlessly reframeable); and environmental/business constraints that don't contradict the problem or the people facing it.
  - Specifically: the anchor is juniors, not superiors — people more deeply vested in the problem than I am, who question me on goal/audience/KPI. I brute-force first, but respect for these people lets me actually take their feedback and refine. Involving more people and taking feedback from juniors is the recipe for my best work — deference to whoever's closer to the problem, not deference to authority.
  - Weakness: I get seduced by good math — passes, workation-coliving pricing before — and end up solving for the business instead of the user. Whenever business and user interest are in tension, I'll build the wrong solution, because I'll bring the business bias in myself regardless. So it's not enough for the environment to be "biased toward the user" against the business (that's still a tug of war) — the business win has to be *structurally, by design and policy, a small contribution of the user's win*, not opposed to it. And the root cause of business/user collision is usually survival mode or growth/profiteering obsession — so the environment must never put me in fight-or-flight, and money/profiteering has to be secondary and near-immaterial given the nature of the transaction.
  - PS, caught myself doing it live: VC (win comes from the startup winning), or a SaaS/commission-model business, both sound like they'd fit the "business win baked into user win" criteria above — but that's jumping to solution before the problem is defined. Same pattern as the first failure logged. Note the instance, don't chase the solution yet.
- What am I afraid of right now, and is it shaping this decision the way fear has shaped past ones?
  - Biggest fear right now: patterns repeating. I'm trying hard to break patterns in life — if they repeat, in any form, it will break me.
  - Revisiting the four open life risks from my Aug 2025 note ("31st year of my life") — the emotional connection, the IC-to-strategic-leader evolution, physical aging, the new life path: Interestingly I have failed at all of them as they were last year. And yet I arrived somewhere else. I have a new someone for that emotional connection, those emotions did remain unrequited. I did evolve well into the strategic leader but I quit the job because the work environment was toxic. Aging, well I did start some good practices there but still is the weakest of all. Life path I was talking about last year, that I didn't pursue but here I am trying something else — again.
  - On the "again": last year's process was also written and sort of disciplined. I guess that was a bit more synthetic while we have worked hard to make this one as authentic as possible.
  - What made it synthetic: it was done with a belief that discipline is what I need. There was some end goal and the process was something I forced on myself that didn't necessarily give me joy.
- What resources — skill, time, money, relationships — do I actually have today, not what I could raise or build?
  - Building simple digital products is right up my alley, but tech has become a commodity — anyone can write software with basic instructions now. Software isn't a moat anymore. I don't have money or network as leverage either. Selling — dicey, unproven. What I do have: years of building products and startups, real learning from real failures, a maturing process. Open question: can that — the ability to find truly interesting, tough problems and an efficient process to get to depth — become the actual moat, in a world where tech itself is cheap?
  - Directions on the table,
    - Obvious ones: founder (again), PM at the intersection of tech and growth, VC (aspirational).
    - Plausibly better fit: venture builder / studio operator — mentoring multiple early founding teams at once using pattern-recognition to de-risk product-market fit before it's built. Structurally closer to my actual shape than plain founder.
  - Caution flag on venture builder/incubator specifically: this has been cooking in my head for a while already — which means it's suspect by my own rule. it's solution-first before the problem is fully defined. Proceed with caution.
- This also makes me realise an interesting thing about myself and the startups I have been building or working at. While I have been trying to work on problems that would change humanity i.e. Self actualisation level, my startups were struggling for basic funds i.e. Physiological needs. The ones I worked at, they might have the basic needs solved but couldn't secure financial stability or a growth path. Safety and security was at risk. The disconnect that I was trying to look at. The imagination vs grasp is pretty clear here.
- I have tried to sit down and grunt it out. But pretty soon it starts showing up that the vision is lost while trying to survive. At the same time, in a lot of cases companies just ended up shutting down before total collapse of me.
- A critical question for me to answer. I guess being able to survive no matter what is the survival mode I personally have been living through. So need to probably build something where tier 1 is largely in control from day 0 and effort each time reduces to moving to next tier or staying put.
 


## 2. The world I live in

- What have I personally observed breaking down, in my own life or the lives of people close to me — not what I've read or theorized?
  - **The commercial system that forces a choice between user health and business survival.** Repeated across every venture I've worked on.
  - Seed Savers Club: farmers economically trapped in unsustainable practices, information gaps
  - goSTOPS: guest experience breaking (social discovery, recommendations, community), staff struggling (check-in, room management), infrastructure cascading failures, business chasing revenue over user need
  - Thrive: I built a delivery engine but chose to solve for business metrics over logistics reality
  - Wider than business models: I want greenery, self-grown food, sustainability, equality — and I live the opposite. City-expansion I hate but feed into, a polluting vehicle, quick commerce, household help only possible because of inequality, media noise I give into instead of the content I'd choose, an LLM habit that costs the climate I claim to care about. Two decades ago it felt like better individual choices were available; now it feels like they aren't.
  
- Of the systems I could look at (social media, dating, e-commerce, institutions, something else) — which one do I have real, current contact with, not a decade-old opinion about?
  - **Commercial/business models in India's startup ecosystem.** The ones that can't hold both user health and business sustainability at once. I've built inside them three times.
  
- What evidence do I have that this system is broken today, right now — not in 2013, not in theory?
  - The moment survival mode or growth pressure kicks in, every system I've worked on forced a choice: user gets what they need, OR business gets sustainable margin. Not both.
  - goSTOPS example: workation-coliving pricing, I watched myself prioritize revenue over what guests actually wanted
  - Pattern: I pick business (trained to, VC trained me, "that's how it works"), user experience collapses, I feel wrong about it
  
- Who is actually harmed by this, specifically, and how would I know if I talked to them?
  - Farmers caught in poverty cycles with unsustainable practices (Seed Savers)
  - Guests paying for experiences that don't deliver (goSTOPS)
  - Staff managing broken workflows (goSTOPS)
  - Logistics operators absorbing margin pressure (Thrive)
  
- What do I believe about why broken systems persist — and is that belief mine, or inherited?
  - Business models are structurally misaligned: they require growth/profiteering as the primary incentive, which forces a collision with user/stakeholder health
  - The business win has to be structurally, by design and policy, a small contribution of the user's win — not opposed to it
  - Most startups and scaling companies in India can't hold both. They don't have business models for it.
  - This is my belief, not inherited: I've watched it happen inside, watched myself make the trade-offs, watched what breaks as a result
  - Older belief of mine, from a 2026-02 note: society is structurally designed to preserve itself, and dependency on it makes broken systems go unquestioned — "the only real freedom is a constant struggle of preserving individuality against the set structure." That reframes "why do broken systems persist" from a business-model-specific answer to a general one: exit/renegotiation, which works on a bad job or relationship, doesn't exist against a system built to resist exactly that. Open question I haven't resolved: is my current read ("I don't have much choice anymore") accurate, or am I still measuring civilizational-scale problems against a personal-contract model where a clean individual choice should exist?
  - Concrete test of the "no exit" claim: even the fantasy exit — detach from society, live remote on bare necessities — still requires startup money and ongoing money. Power itself doesn't escape this: it's held/maintained *in order to* convert to money for whoever holds it or installed them. Money is the thing every resource is either valued in, tied to, or eventually converted into. The only near-exception — wildlife, forests — stops being an exception the moment the alternative is monetizing them directly (poaching, extraction). So "I don't have much choice anymore" reads less like resignation and more like an accurate description: there's no substrate outside money to stand on to renegotiate from.
  - Beyond money I think power, control, respect i.e. the human pyramid is also very interesting. While often closely correlated with Money but this has its own parallel structure. Often power/control/respect competes with or is replacable with money.
  - Relationships are another core behind humanity. We are designed to be social beings and there is that thing about sexual needs. Power, Money and Relationships these 3 axis sort of also probably fit somewhere in maslow's hierarchy. So as of now I have two frameworks. Resources, coordination and cohesion. And money, power and relationships. And they map nicely to each other. Resources = money, power = coordination and relationships = cohesion.
  - There was a time Japan as a society was reaching for self actualisation. As a society there are times when people are not meeting their fundamental needs due to extreme poverty. Cohesion and Coordination doesn't matter. Just access to resources. As we grow as an economy, there is more investment in building infra and defence. And then international relationships and standing. We start caring about how the world sees us. Once we have all of it, we want to innovate. Move frontiers of humanity. Like US has been doing. Or we try to perfect our craft like Japan was doing at a point. So as a society too, we push the frontiers of humanity.
  - Legacy seeking as a society is as delusional as legacy seeking as a human being. Because societies too, die. But then self actualisation as a way of life and not as a way of leaving a legacy behind is something different.
  - Now it feels like I almost have a framework in form of Maslow's hierarchy of needs at an individual level scaling upto humanity. I can try to reach for frontier problems for humanity through my problems. One such problem is health.

The full food chain (history → mechanism → gap → requirements → experiments) → `problems/health/food.md`. Published artifact → `food-frontier-article.md`. Process solidified → `playbook.md`.
