# Playbook — Frontier Problems Through My Own Life

*The process, as it actually worked on 2026-07-11 (food). Update it every time a run teaches something.*

## The core frame

Maslow's hierarchy at the individual level scales up to humanity. Frontier problems for humanity are reached **through my own problems** — a problem qualifies only if it already shows up at my level of the hierarchy. This closes the imagination-vs-grasp gap by construction: the grasp (my life, tier 1) is the entry point of the imagination (humanity, tier 5), not a competitor to it.

## Ground rules (before any run)

- **Authenticity test:** the process must give joy in itself, with no predetermined destination. The synthetic version — discipline as self-prescription, forced toward an end goal — is the known failure mode. The day a session feels like an obligation to reach "the answer," pause the goal, not the practice.
- **Pace:** slow, incremental, daily discipline. One step per sitting is a complete sitting.
- **Solution pulls get logged, never chased.** Writing "what is required is X" is the last problem-side sentence. Note the pull, return to the problem.
- **Verbatim capture:** thinking goes to Slate and the docs in my own words. Recall before responding on any topic I may have prior thinking on — full-paragraph queries, per new topic.
- **Anchors:** people closer to the problem than me get to question goal/audience/KPI. Field conversations are where they live.

## The loop

1. **Notice.** A problem reaches me (I don't go hunting). Check Slate for recurrence — a problem that has reached me across years, independently, is the strongest signal. (Food/medical trust recurred across 2017, 2019, 2021, 2023, 2026 notes.)
2. **Map.** Run the domain through my Maslow tiers, personally — pure lived observation, no theory. Grade each tier: living it / observed it / reflection only. Tiers without real contact fail the filter.
3. **History.** How was this domain's last frontier solved? What incentive machinery did the solution leave running? The previous solution's side effects are usually the current problem.
4. **Self-resolution test.** Does the visible recovery match the damage in *type* (structural damage needs structural repair — awareness decays, incentives compound)? Is the recovery reaching everyone the loss reached (a universal loss with a class-skewed recovery does not self-resolve)?
5. **Mechanism.** Why do current fixes underdeliver? Follow attention, trust, and incentives. Ask what the consumer's cheapest move is — that's what wins, independent of truth.
6. **Gap statement.** One sentence. Test it: does it explain every symptom found in steps 3–5?
7. **Requirements, not solutions.** What would any solution have to deliver, and in what currency (money vs time vs attention)? Stop there.
8. **Verify.** Research pass on every historical/factual claim (parallel agents, cited sources). Trace every popular statistic to its original source and funder — problem statements arrive pre-shaped by whoever profits from them ("73% protein deficient" → Danone).
9. **Live it.** Solve the problem for myself with the best the world currently offers. Whatever I still can't solve is, by construction, a real gap — personally verified as unsolved. This is simultaneously discovery and fixing my own tier.
10. **Talk.** Casual, non-leading field conversations across strata and trust-channels (not just my bubble). Ask about yesterday's plate, not about problems. Listening targets defined in advance; conversation succeeds when *they* talk longer.
11. **Publish.** Crystallize into an artifact (article) with verified claims, personal anecdote toned down to relatable.
12. **Conclude.** Explicit check: did this bring a large-imagination problem into my grasp? Log the conclusion, pick the next tier or domain, go to 1.

## Failure modes to watch (from Section 1 of the notebook)

- Solution-first, then reverse-engineered problem (the 2023 medical note jumped diagnosis→Web3 blueprint in one page; the solution aged badly, the problem didn't age at all).
- Partial listening — pattern-matching people's words to an idea I already hold.
- Primed re-derivation: when the framework "independently" arrives at an old favorite (Seed Savers territory), ask honestly whether it's validation or priming.
- Business-math seduction — if a run starts optimizing the model instead of the user, re-read notebook Section 1.

## Documentation architecture

```
fph/
├── notebook.md                  # working journal: who I am, the world, the method — verbatim thinking
├── playbook.md                  # this file: the solidified process
├── problems/
│   ├── <domain>.md              # domain overview: tier mapping, tier status, cross-cutting insights
│   └── <domain>/
│       └── <sub-problem>.md     # full chain: history → mechanism → gap → requirements → experiments
├── <artifact>.md                # published pieces (e.g. food-frontier-article.md)
└── slate_use_case.txt
```

- **notebook.md** stays the thinking surface — raw, verbatim, chronological within sections.
- **problems/** docs are the distilled state of each problem — updated after runs, safe to hand to someone else.
- Slate holds everything verbatim with provenance; docs hold the current structured picture. When they disagree, Slate is the record, docs are the map.
