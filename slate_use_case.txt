  The idea is sound and it's a known move — Feynman's version of "one thing
  about everything" is holography-of-knowledge; the self-help version is "you
  are a fractal of the systems you're embedded in." Practically, it works as a
  filter, not a discovery engine:

  - Your close, deeply-understood world (family, goSTOPS, your own
  money/health/relationship patterns) is a high-fidelity sample of larger
  systems, not a separate small thing. A problem you feel acutely at home (e.g.
  decision paralysis under scarcity-fear — you've already named this pattern) is
  very likely a scaled-down instance of a problem millions face, just with
  better instrumentation because you're the sensor.
  - The leverage move: pick problems where (1) you have unusually deep, embodied
  data most outsiders lack, and (2) the mechanism generalizes (psychological,
  economic, structural — not idiosyncratic to your household). That's the
  intersection of mission-fit and leverage you already use as a filter.
  - Risk: this can become navel-gazing if you stop at description. The
  universe-is-one-system frame only pays off if you go one level up from "I feel
  X" to "what structural condition produces X in me and in people like me,"
  then test that against outside data.

  So: yes, usable — but the test isn't "can I find a problem by looking at
  myself," it's "can I find the general mechanism underneath the personal
  instance, and does someone else's data confirm it's not just you."

⏺ Full picture, from a 2017 note "Social Contracts: Why we fail to abide by
  them?":

  Core thesis: the fraud isn't when you break a contract — it's the moment you
  enter it. Every compromise (job, degree, relationship) is you defrauding
  yourself, betting you can live with less than what you actually wanted. You
  can't. Your "innate identity" resurfaces eventually and forces the reckoning.

  Mechanism: people don't abandon the compromise once made — they pretend to
  fulfill it (study without curiosity, work without effort, love without love)
  while collecting its rewards. A society where everyone's pretending becomes
  one where integrity is impossible and "the people with conscience always
  suffer."

  Why people enter bad contracts anyway: "the end justifies means" —
  financial/existential pressure, plus self-deception that the bad option "is
  the best we deserve."

  Prediction: no one wins the race to the bottom; a society built on broken
  contracts eventually fails.

  Adjacent concepts: Relationship Identity Erosion (2020, "Blue Walls" note) —
  same mechanism at individual scale: self-erasure, rationalization,

⏺ From your 2017 note "Life: Survival," the reframe is: survival isn't physical
  continuation, it's identity preservation. That single move drives everything
  else in the note:

  Suicide reframed — under the standard definition, suicide is anti-survival,
  therefore pathology. Under your definition, it's a rational (if extreme) act
  when "the very idea of existence is threatened by continuance of life" — i.e.,
  staying alive would require living as someone you no longer recognize as
  yourself. The crisis isn't despair in the abstract; it's a specific identity
  being falsified with no path back to who you were.

  Extended self — you note this can even extend to people close to you being
  folded into "self," which is why in the worst cases someone might try to
  protect people close to them by ending their lives too, not out of hatred but
  because those people are experienced as part of the same self under threat.

  Temporary vs. core identities — you draw a survival mechanism here: humans can
  spin up temporary identities to cope with extreme situations (your example:
  Stockholm Syndrome, soldiers in war). These are supposed to be disposable —
  shed once the situation ends, ideally with help ("proper counselling"). The
  danger case is when the temporary identity doesn't get destroyed and becomes
  load-bearing — the soldier who never leaves the war-identity behind and can't
  function in ordinary life afterward.

  Why "full crisis, not local adjustment" — your model treats identity as one
  interlocking system, not independent modules. A local threat (losing a job, a
  belief being disproven) is absorbable if it doesn't touch the "most
  fundamental identity." But when the threatened piece IS foundational, there's
  no partial patch available — the whole structure has to reorganize, hence
  "radical life change" rather than incremental correction.

  Tie-back to today's thread: this is the ceiling case of the compromise/erosion
  pattern you've been tracing. Blue Walls describes slow erosion; this note
  describes what happens if erosion ever reaches something foundational — the
  system stops accepting small compromises and forces a reorganization instead.
