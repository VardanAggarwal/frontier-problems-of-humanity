# We Fixed Hunger and Broke Dinner

*Why eating well is still an unsolved problem — and why it won't solve itself.*

---

Try a small experiment. Ask anyone — a colleague, your mother, the person delivering your lunch — what they actually ate yesterday. Not what they aspire to eat. Everything, start to finish.

You'll hear a strikingly narrow list. In vegetarian India, paneer has quietly become *the* protein. A couple of daals rotate through the week. The local legumes your grandmother knew by name — the kulthi, the moth, the khesari — have vanished so completely that most of us can't tell whether we miss them. Fewer than 10% of Indian households report eating any millets at all ([ICRISAT/IDEAS for India](https://www.ideasforindia.in/topics/agriculture/farm-to-fork-an-overview-of-millet-supply-chains-in-india)).

This didn't happen because we stopped caring about food. It happened because, twice in sixty years, the menu was decided somewhere other than the kitchen.

## The first shrinking: the plate was engineered

India's Green Revolution solved a real and desperate problem. After the famine scares of 1965–66, high-yielding wheat and rice varieties — backed by fertiliser, irrigation, and an entire policy apparatus — pulled the country back from dependence on food aid. That aid itself had teeth: under the US PL-480 program, President Johnson personally released wheat shipments month-to-month, explicitly conditioned on India adopting agricultural reforms — the "short-tether" policy ([Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/book/3199/chapter/144125512)). The Rockefeller and Ford Foundations funded the science; Indian planners like C. Subramaniam and M.S. Swaminathan, staring at empty granaries, actively embraced it. Nobody was villainous here. Everybody was solving the era's frontier problem: calories.

But the machinery built to solve calories never switched off. Minimum support prices, assured government procurement, APMC mandis, and a public distribution system that stocked its fair-price shops overwhelmingly with rice and wheat — every layer of incentive pointed at the same two crops. Even today, the Food Corporation of India procures on the order of 266 lakh tonnes of wheat and 775 lakh tonnes of paddy a year, while millets have no comparable procurement infrastructure at all ([DFPD](https://dfpd.gov.in/procurement-policy/en); [PRS India](https://prsindia.org/theprsblog/msp-and-public-procurement)).

The results are stark. Land under millets fell from roughly 37 million hectares in the mid-1960s to under 15 million by 2016-17. Wheat area more than tripled. Rural consumption of jowar fell 73% in just the two decades after 1991 ([Journal of Ethnic Foods](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s42779-019-0011-9); [ICRISAT](https://www.ideasforindia.in/topics/agriculture/farm-to-fork-an-overview-of-millet-supply-chains-in-india)). Fertiliser use went from under a million tonnes before the mid-60s to nearly 13 million tonnes by the early 90s ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10113-025-02395-9)).

And this wasn't only an Indian story. Globally, corporate consolidation achieved by procurement contract what Indian policy achieved by MSP: four firms control over 60% of the world's commercial seed market; four traders have historically handled 70–90% of global grain trade ([Oxfam](https://policy-practice.oxfam.org/resources/cereal-secrets-the-worlds-largest-grain-traders-and-global-agriculture-237131/)). Between 1961 and 2009, national food supplies worldwide became about 36% *more similar* to each other — everyone converging on wheat, rice, maize, soy, and palm oil while sorghum, millets, and root crops receded ([Khoury et al., PNAS](https://www.pnas.org/doi/full/10.1073/pnas.1313490111)). Around 30 crops now supply about 95% of humanity's calories.

The frontier problem of the 1960s was solved brilliantly. The solution's side effect was that the farmer and the city professional lost the same foods at the same time — one stopped growing them, the other stopped seeing them.

## The second shrinking: the kitchen emptied

The next contraction needed no policy at all. Economic growth did it.

Time in the kitchen collapsed while food from outside became one tap away. India's urban elite now spend about half their food budget on packaged food, dining out, and delivery — up from ~41% a decade ago; the mid-income segment's processed-food share jumped from roughly 16% to 25% ([Business Standard/MoSPI](https://www.business-standard.com/economy/news/indians-spending-more-on-packaged-food-eating-out-as-home-cooking-declines-124041000308_1.html)). Two apps handle over 90% of organized food delivery across 500+ cities. Ultra-processed food retail in India grew roughly forty-fold between 2006 and 2019, one of the fastest expansions anywhere in the world ([Think Global Health](https://www.thinkglobalhealth.org/article/curbing-indias-ultra-processed-foods)).

Restaurant economics then finished what farm economics started: a commercial kitchen survives on a handful of versatile, storable ingredients. The outside menu is broad in dishes and narrow in foods. Order vegetarian protein anywhere in India and watch paneer arrive, again.

Notice who this touches: everyone. The IT employee ordering dinner and the delivery partner bringing it are both eating a growing share of their meals from the same shrunken ingredient pool. The narrowing of the plate may be one of the few problems in India that is genuinely class-blind.

## The problem changed shape

Here's what makes this moment different from 1965: the problem is no longer calories. India solved calories. What the narrowed plate left behind is what nutritionists call hidden hunger — and it is anything but hidden in the data.

NFHS-5 (2019–21) found **57% of Indian women and 67% of children under five anemic** — and both numbers went *up* from the previous survey round, through years of rising incomes ([NFHS-5](https://factly.in/data-nfhs-5-findings-reveal-increase-in-anaemia-prevalence-in-children-women-across-most-states/); [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11476725/)). The national CNNS survey puts B12 deficiency at 14–31% and folate deficiency at 23–36% of children and adolescents, depending on age group ([CNNS/PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10346745/)). Meta-analyses across dozens of Indian studies consistently place vitamin D deficiency somewhere between two-thirds and 90% of the population ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6060930/)). If the last century's food problem was scaled nationally, this one scales even better — anemia doesn't check your income bracket.

Protein tells a subtler story, and it's worth telling carefully. The average Indian gets about 55.6 g of protein a day — near-adequate in *quantity* by ICMR-NIN standards. The real gap is *quality*: cereals supply nearly 50% of India's protein against a recommended 32%, displacing the pulses, dairy, and eggs that provide complete amino-acid profiles ([CEEW](https://www.ceew.in/press-releases/indians-are-getting-protein-poor-quality%E2%80%94nearly-50-comes-cereals-ceew)). That, of course, is the Green Revolution's signature written directly into our bloodstream — we grew cereals, so we eat cereals, so even our protein comes from cereals.

But notice what happened to this nuanced problem on its way to the public. The claim you've actually heard — "73% of Indians are protein deficient" — traces back to an IMRB survey of 1,800 urban respondents **commissioned by Danone India**, deployed through a marketing construct called "Protein Week" ([Business Standard](https://www.business-standard.com/article/pti-stories/73-indian-diets-protein-deficient-study-117072701307_1.html); [Danone](https://www.danone.in/press/the-protein-week-2020-danone-india-and-cii-take-a-pledgeto-sensitize-indians-on-the-role-of-protein-in-buildingimmunity/)). The deficiency is real; the number everyone quotes was manufactured by a company that sells protein. India's protein supplements market is on its way from $1 billion to $2.7 billion ([Grand View Research](https://www.grandviewresearch.com/horizon/outlook/protein-supplements-market/india)). Even our problem statements now arrive pre-shaped by whoever profits from them.

And how did the urban middle class discover its deficiencies? Not through the food system — through the diagnostics industry. Full-body checkup packages are growing 22–28% a year, the fastest segment of a diagnostics market headed from ~$40 billion toward $124 billion ([Grand View Research](https://www.grandviewresearch.com/horizon/outlook/health-check-up-market/india); [ResearchAndMarkets](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20250527862915/en/India-Diagnostic-Laboratories-Market-Report-2025-Market-Set-to-Double-by-2030-Driven-by-Healthcare-Awareness-Tech-Advancements---ResearchAndMarkets.com)). A ₹1,500 blood panel became a consumer product, and a generation met its vitamin D levels for the first time. Keep that fact in hand — it matters enormously in a moment.

## The recovery that isn't one

Here is where the story is supposed to turn. Awareness is rising. 2023 was the UN's International Year of Millets; the government rebranded them "Shree Anna" and put them on G20 banquet menus. Organic is a category in every store. Cooking is aspirational content again.

Look closer, though. India's entire millet market is worth about $116 million — a rounding error in the food economy — and its growth is explicitly urban, health-conscious, and premium: ready-to-eat millet snacks and branded flours, not millets returning to everyday thalis ([Deep Market Insights](https://deepmarketinsights.com/vista/insights/millet-market/india)). Production rises for export and prestige while fewer than one in ten households actually eats the stuff.

This is the pattern that should worry us: **the loss was universal, but the recovery is stratified.** Millets left everyone's plate; they are returning only to plates that can pay a premium. A universal problem with a class-skewed recovery does not resolve on its own — it just becomes invisible to the people writing about it.

## Why stories keep beating labels

There's a deeper reason the awareness wave under-delivers, and it lives in the gap between two kinds of value.

When you eat good food, some of its value is verifiable on the spot — taste, freshness, satisfaction. But the part we're all suddenly anxious about — nutrition, purity, long-term health — is *imperceptible at the moment of consumption*. You cannot taste a protein deficiency. You cannot see whether milk is A2. Your body will not send you a notification.

Where value can't be verified, narrative fills the vacuum. And narrative has a decisive cost advantage: reading labels, comparing sources, and researching claims costs the one thing nobody has — attention. Health sits permanently in the "important but never urgent" file. A brand story resolves that tension beautifully: it lets you pay a small premium and feel you've done your bit. The extra ₹40 isn't buying nutrition. It's buying absolution.

Consider the evidence from India's most instructive category: milk.

The fear: *everything is adulterated.* The reality: when FSSAI tested 6,432 milk samples across the country in 2018, **0.19%** — twelve samples — showed actual adulteration; 93% were fully safe (quality issues like aflatoxin traces existed in a minority, but the poison-panic narrative found no support) ([FSSAI](https://www.fssai.gov.in/upload/press_release/2019/10/5da973ffaefcfPress_Release_Milk_Survey_Report_18_10_2019.pdf)). Yet the fear is so commercially potent that an entire premium category — A2 milk, at roughly double the regular price — was built on it, despite the European Food Safety Authority finding no established cause-and-effect between A1 milk protein and any disease back in 2009 ([EFSA review](https://www.foodnavigator.com/Article/2009/02/09/EFSA-reveals-milk-protein-safety-conclusions/)). When India's food regulator moved to strip A1/A2 claims from packaging in August 2024, the advisory was withdrawn within five days for "further consultation" ([Business Today](https://www.businesstoday.in/latest/economy/story/fssai-withdraws-advisory-on-removal-of-claims-of-a1-a2-milk-from-packaging-443111-2024-08-27)).

Read that sequence again. The fear is exaggerated, the science is unsupported, the premium is 2x — and the claims remain on the shelf. Narrative didn't just beat transparency; the referee left the field.

It isn't an isolated case. India's Supreme Court spent 2024 dragging Patanjali through contempt proceedings over repeated misleading health advertisements. A viral video pointing out the sugar content of Bournvita ended with the government quietly ordering e-commerce platforms to abolish the "health drink" category — which, it turned out, had never been legally defined at all.

Each stratum, meanwhile, copes with the trust vacuum using the currency it has. The time-rich go backward: making ghee at home, buying spices whole, reconstructing older supply chains. The money-rich pick a premium brand and stop thinking. Everyone else buys the cheapest and waits for quality to become price-competitive. Nobody — at any income level — can actually *verify* anything. The best any of us do is choose whose story to believe. I say this without exemption: my own grocery cart runs on a curation service's quality claims I have never once checked.

## The missing instrument

So: awareness exists. Capital exists — brands are investing heavily in exactly this anxiety. Why call food an unsolved frontier problem at all?

Because the force everyone is counting on to fix the plate — health consciousness — runs on a feedback signal that does not exist.

If the next food transition is supposed to be driven by people eating better for their health, then somewhere, someone has to be able to *see* their health respond to their food. Today, almost nobody can. The outcome we ultimately care about — healthspan, longevity — plays out over decades and is confounded by everything else in life.

Now recall the fact from earlier: the measurement layer is actually being built. Those full-body checkups growing 25% a year are exactly the instrument this problem needs — deficiency panels, biomarkers, numbers that move in months rather than decades. But look at where the instrument lives: inside the diagnostics and hospital system, the part of Indian life people already find least trustworthy and most inconvenient. Your blood panel and your kitchen have never exchanged a word. The lab tells you your B12 is low and hands you a PDF; no part of that loop reaches what's on your plate next Tuesday. The fuel gauge exists — it's just installed in a different car.

So the precise statement of the gap: **the metric exists but is disconnected from the eating loop, and the eating loop is connected only to narrative.**

The consequences of this missing instrument explain every symptom above:

- **Claims go unpunished** — with no outcome data, nothing ever contradicts a brand story. The A2 premium is unfalsifiable at the individual level.
- **Verification stays unaffordable** — without a number to check, "verifying" means doing research, and research costs attention nobody has.
- **Healthy habits churn** — this is the quiet killer. Behavioral research consistently shows that self-monitoring and feedback are among the strongest predictors of dietary adherence, and that intervention dropout runs around 40% within the first year ([Burke et al., systematic reviews](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3268700/)). People don't abandon healthy eating because they stop believing in it. They abandon it because effort with no visible signal is indistinguishable from wasted effort. They tried; nothing measurable moved; they receded.

The Green Revolution succeeded because its feedback was immediate and legible: yield per hectare, granaries filling, famine receding. You could *see* it work. The health-consciousness revolution is being asked to run on faith.

## What solving it would have to look like

I don't have the solution, and I'm suspicious of anyone who reaches for one this early. But the problem statement implies its own requirements, and they're worth stating plainly:

1. **An outcome signal.** Trying a better diet has to produce something an ordinary person can watch move — within months, not decades, and without becoming a hobby. The raw material already exists in the checkup boom; what's missing is any connection between the panel and the plate.
2. **A trust layer owned by the eater's side.** Every existing "validation" — labels, certifications, brand stories, influencer endorsements — is produced by the seller's side of the table. The one mechanism that historically worked, the family doctor and the known farmer, was trust embodied in a relationship, costing the consumer nothing to use.
3. **It must charge money, never time.** Attention is the binding constraint at every income level. Any solution that asks people to research, track, or study has already failed. Small money, zero time — that's the design envelope.

Whether this gets built by a company, a public institution, or something we don't have a name for yet, I don't know. What I do know is that the last time the plate changed, it changed because an entire architecture of incentives made the new behavior the default. Awareness was never the mechanism; it was the marketing.

The frontier problem in food is not growing better food, and it is not informing people harder. It is closing the loop between what we eat and what it does to us — cheaply, trustably, and without asking for a minute of anyone's day.

Until that loop closes, the menu will keep being written by whoever tells the best story.
